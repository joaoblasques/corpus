TL;DR: OSTEP (ostep.org) is the canonical undergraduate OS textbook by Remzi Arpaci-Dussouri and Andrea Arpaci-Dussouri. Free online. Organized around three "easy" pieces: virtualization, concurrency, and persistence. Short focused chapters, each stating a "crux of the problem" before solving it.

## Overview

**Operating Systems: Three Easy Pieces** (OSTEP) by Remzi H. Arpaci-Dussouri and Andrea C. Arpaci-Dussouri. Available free at ostep.org. Title is an homage to Feynman's physics lecture notes. The book is organized around three themes:

1. **Virtualization** — the OS's abstraction of physical hardware into multiple virtual resources (processes, address spaces)
2. **Concurrency** — threads, locks, condition variables, and the challenges of parallel execution
3. **Persistence** — durable storage: file systems, journaling, SSDs, RAID, distributed file systems

Each chapter presents a "crux of the problem" (the central design challenge) before developing solutions. The companion OS is **xv6**, a simple Unix-like OS used for labs.

## Pedagogical Approach

**Crux pattern**: every chapter starts with "THE CRUX: HOW TO [DO X]?" — forces the reader to hold the design problem before seeing the solution [^src1].

**Three easy pieces metaphor**: virtualization, concurrency, and persistence are "three easy pieces" only in that they are the three fundamental problems — each is actually quite hard [^src1].

**Learn from history**: MLFQ is cited as a prime example of how OS algorithms learn from past behavior to predict the future — an approach pervasive in computer science (branch predictors, caching algorithms) [^src2].

## Companion Lab Environment

**xv6**: a simple, modern re-implementation of Unix V6, used for labs. Available on GitHub. Labs include building a shell, implementing system calls, adding schedulers, and extending file systems [^src3].
