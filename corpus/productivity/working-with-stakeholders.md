# Working with Stakeholders

**TL;DR** — Technical work serves a business, which means internal stakeholders. A key stakeholder "can either become your #1 fan and ally... or a massive pain in your side." The lever that decides which: reliably delivering on agreed scope, then proactively managing the relationship after delivery [^src1][^src2].

## Stakeholders vs. customers

- Most engineers entered the field for the technical work, "not because we want to sit around in meetings all day" — but the reality is you serve a business with internal stakeholders who rely on your output [^src1].
- The source frames the dynamic across three lenses: **stakeholders vs. customers**, **short-term efficiency gains**, and **long-term career opportunities** — i.e., a good stakeholder relationship is both an execution multiplier and a career asset [^src1].

> Note: [^src1] is a short pointer to a YouTube video; the framing above is its stated scope. The mechanics below come from the companion source [^src2].

## Trust compounds faster than brilliance

A stakeholder remembers two things: whether your work was good, and whether you delivered when you said you would — and "the second one compounds faster than the first" [^src2].

- Shipping clean, scoped work on time builds a **track record of reliability**: "you get pulled into the room where the decision is being made, because someone already knows you can be trusted to close things out" [^src2].
- The hidden cost of over-polishing is relational: a week spent past scope is a week of relationship capital *not* built on the next project [^src2].

## Manage the relationship after delivery

What converts a *delivered* project into *trust* is what happens after "done": reading when the stakeholder has stopped hunting for gaps, asking only what is **critical** rather than inviting scope creep, and setting clear expectations for post-launch support. The mechanics of all three — the feedback shift, the "is there anything critical missing?" closing question, and the post-launch contract — live in [Shipping and Scope](/productivity/shipping-and-scope.md).

The relationship point layered on top of those mechanics: "a stakeholder who knows what to expect from you after launch trusts you more" — both the engineer who disappears and the one who commits to everything indefinitely damage the relationship [^src2].

## Proactive alignment: close the information gap yourself

"Every surprise project, urgent pivot, and 'nobody told us' moment are your damn fault. That's a business alignment problem, and it starts with you" [^src3].

The business always moves faster than it communicates — that is not a dysfunction, it is structural. The difference between a team that firefights all year and a team that delivers is one proactive conversation, held *before* the pressure is on [^src3].

**The reactivity trap**: handling fires looks impressive short-term — you appear responsive, indispensable. But "nobody above you sees a leader. They see someone who is very good at cleaning up messes." Every urgent pivot your team absorbs without warning costs approximately two weeks of actual work in retooling, re-scoping, and reprioritisation — and that cost "disappears into your team's capacity" invisibly [^src3].

**Alignment is just a conversation**: not a formal process, a recurring meeting, or a color-coded stakeholder map. It is "you walking over to your PM and asking 'what's keeping you up at night for the next quarter?'" — ten minutes, held early enough to shape what's coming [^src3].

**The timing asymmetry**: "One business alignment conversation in March is worth more than any amount of sprint planning in September. By September the decisions are made, the timelines are set, and your team is already behind" [^src3].

**Reputation compounding**: "The person known for calm, prepared delivery gets pulled into bigger problems. The person known for handling chaos gets handed more chaos." Reputation around *prevention* (rather than *execution*) is the career unlock for senior ICs and managers alike [^src3].

This connects to the Map Is Not Territory model (see [Mental Models](/productivity/mental-models.md)): the team that firefights is relying on a roadmap that was already stale before it was written. Proactive alignment is the act of checking the terrain before committing to the map.

## The human layer of technical work

Technical failure is less common than human failure. "You can be the best engineer on the team and still see a project struggle, not because the technology failed, but because communication did" [^src4].

**Psychological safety as a team infrastructure** [^src4]:
- People become defensive or protective when their work is "picked apart" — even well-meaning feedback that "takes little jabs" shrinks psychological safety. A team full of shrinking people "produces the minimum, not the best work."
- Safety is built differently across cultures: humour works in some contexts (Australia) while more formal directness works in others (USA corporate). What matters is shared understanding of the norms, not adherence to one template.

**Empathy as data, not softness** [^src4]:
- "Empathy is not softness. It is information." Reading someone's stress load, their fear about a project, their unstated priorities enables *better* decisions — "not kinder ones."
- Colette Molteni's PACE System (Stoicism + emotional intelligence + human-AI partnership): feel what you feel, but choose what you show and when. "That is not inauthenticity. It is maturity" — applied Stoicism as emotional calibration under pressure.

**Common mistakes of technically-strong people** [^src4]:
- Assuming cross-functional colleagues understand technical context ("Dashboard needs to be fixed" means the data at the root source needs fixing, not the display — knowing this saves the panic spiral).
- Solving before listening: body language (open palms, relaxed eyes), genuine questions, and "asking what they want before presenting your solution" do more repair than any technical fix.
- Neglecting the after: when an interjection in a meeting inadvertently undermines a colleague, follow up directly afterward — "not to apologise for helping, but to acknowledge their expertise and ask how they'd prefer to handle it next time."

**Influence without politics**: be easy to work with, emotionally steady, and genuinely helpful — "not kinder decisions, better ones." Those who get promoted are not always the most technically brilliant; they are the ones who can be trusted to make the room work [^src4].

## Applying idea-quality filters to your own backlog

The same filter applied to stakeholders' requests should apply to your own ideas — but "when the idea is yours, something shifts. The filter goes quiet" [^src5].

**Four gates before committing any team time** [^src5]:

1. **Start with the pain**: Is it measurable when unsolved, or merely an irritant? Does the *org* feel it, or just you?
2. **Research**: Is this the standard? Is this the *most load-bearing* pain to solve, or is there something ahead of it in priority?
3. **Generate alternatives**: If you enter solution mode with the one answer you already want to build, "you're defending an idea, not evaluating a problem." Require at least two alternatives.
4. **Talk to the people it affects**: Are they willing to sponsor this? "A pain that nobody will prioritize is a pain the org has decided to live with."

**The sequencing fix**: run discovery calls bi-weekly across the org; listen for plans and frustrations. "The pain comes first, from the source, and then you decide whether it's worth solving." Sourcing pain externally before generating solutions prevents confirmation bias (going looking for evidence of a problem you already invented) [^src5].
