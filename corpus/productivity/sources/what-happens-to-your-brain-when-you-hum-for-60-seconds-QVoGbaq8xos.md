# What Happens to YOUR BRAIN When You Hum for 60 Seconds?

> **Source** (YouTube · The Feynman Way · 2026-04-07). [watch on YouTube](https://youtu.be/QVoGbaq8xos) · [transcript](../../../raw/youtube/youtube-QVoGbaq8xos-what-happens-to-your-brain-when-you-hum-for-60-seconds.md)

**TL;DR**: A 60-second hum triggers five simultaneous physiological mechanisms — sinus nitric oxide delivery, vagal stimulation, enforced exhale-dominant breathing, bone-conducted vestibular input, and CSF oscillation — via acoustic resonance and bone conduction. The first three are well-evidenced; the last two are physically plausible but not yet directly confirmed.

---

## 1. Anatomy: paranasal sinuses as resonance chambers

The skull contains four pairs of air-filled chambers — **paranasal sinuses** — hollowed into bone: maxillary (behind cheeks, largest), frontal (behind forehead), ethmoid (between eyes), and sphenoid (deepest, center of head). Each connects to the nasal passage through a narrow opening called the **ostium**.[^1]

These are not vestigial drainage passages. They are **Helmholtz resonators** — bony cavities with specific volumes, geometries, and resonant frequencies determined by the air column and ostium dimensions.[^1] Humming vibration propagates through bone at 3,000–4,000 m/s (9–12× faster than through air), reaching every sinus simultaneously via bone conduction through the cranial vault.[^1]

Humming is uniquely effective because it is "the only vocal activity that holds a single frequency long enough for the sinus cavities to reach full resonance."[^1] Speech changes frequency with every syllable; singing changes pitch with every note; only humming sustains one frequency for the full resonance response.

**Optimal pitch**: a relaxed, mid-range note. Maxillary sinuses resonate at approximately 200–350 Hz. Male humming typically falls 100–200 Hz; female 200–400 Hz — naturally closer to maxillary resonance, which may explain higher nasal NO output observed in some female-subject studies.[^1] The natural, unstrained note is already close to the individual's sinus resonant frequency and requires no deliberate calibration.[^1]

## 2. Mechanism 1: Nitric oxide delivery (strong evidence)

**Sinus epithelium continuously produces nitric oxide (NO)** via constitutive nitric oxide synthase. During quiet breathing, NO diffuses slowly through the narrow ostium; the ostium restricts passive diffusion, so NO accumulates in the cavity.[^1]

Humming creates oscillating air pressure inside the sinuses, acting as a mechanical pump: each oscillation flushes NO-enriched air out through the ostium and draws fresh air in, stimulating further production.[^1]

**Weitzberg and Lundberg (Karolinska Institute)** measured nasal NO output during humming versus quiet breathing and found a **15-fold increase** — from ~0 to 3 ppm in exhaled nasal air.[^1] This is a magnitude increase, not a percentage.

Maniscalco and colleagues (European Respiratory Journal) found that "the frequency and amplitude of humming correlated with nasal nitric oxide output, louder humming and frequencies closer to sinus resonance produced higher levels."[^1]

Once inhaled, NO crosses the alveolar membrane into pulmonary capillary blood → activates soluble guanylate cyclase in vascular smooth muscle → increases cyclic GMP → relaxes smooth muscle → dilates pulmonary vasculature. The video notes this is "the identical molecular mechanism of sildenafil, the pharmaceutical vasodilator that generates billions in annual revenue."[^1] Pulmonary vasodilation increases capillary surface area at the alveolar membrane, improving oxygen diffusion per breath.[^1]

**Caveat**: the vasodilatory effect from humming is mild and does not approach pharmaceutical concentrations; it does not replace medical nitric oxide therapy for conditions like neonatal pulmonary hypertension or ARDS.[^1]

**Antimicrobial side-effect**: NO inhibits bacterial and fungal growth. Studies of humming in chronic sinusitis patients showed improved sinus ventilation and reduced infection frequency.[^1]

## 3. Mechanism 2: Vagal stimulation (established evidence)

The vagus nerve runs directly alongside the larynx, separated from vibrating vocal cords by millimeters of tissue. Sustained humming mechanically stimulates the vagus through direct physical vibration of the nerve via mechanosensitive ion channels.[^1]

Bone conduction additionally delivers the vibration to the vagus through the temporal bone (along the nerve's intracranial course), creating **dual delivery**: local at the larynx and remote through the skull. Only humming delivers continuous bone-conducted vibration to the vagus through both pathways for the full duration of a note.[^1]

Observable autonomic effect: heart rate drops ~2–5 bpm within 15–20 seconds; heart rate variability increases; autonomic balance shifts toward parasympathetic dominance.[^1] No conscious effort is required — the vibration stimulates the nerve, the nerve responds.

This is a third distinct vagal activation pathway, separate from: (a) baroreceptor feedback from slow breathing, and (b) trigeminal-vagal reflex from cold water on the face.[^1]

---

## 4. Mechanism 3: Enforced exhale-dominant breathing (physics, not hypothesis)

Humming can only occur during exhalation — vocal cords require exhaled air flowing past them to vibrate. A natural hum cycle therefore produces an exhale-to-inhale ratio of approximately **3:1 to 4:1**, with the exhale extended to 8–12 seconds and the inhale remaining 2–3 seconds.[^1]

This ratio maximally shifts autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance via the **baroreceptor reflex**: slight blood pressure increase during extended exhalation is detected by aortic arch and carotid sinus baroreceptors, triggering vagal output to slow the heart.[^1]

"Clinical breathing protocols… all of them are trying to achieve the exhale-dominant ratio that humming produces automatically."[^1] Box breathing, 4-7-8, coherence breathing all target this ratio but require counting and weeks of practice. Humming enforces it with no instruction on the first attempt.[^1]

The net effect: two vagal pathways fire simultaneously — direct mechanical nerve vibration and baroreceptor-mediated exhale activation — producing a combined parasympathetic shift greater than either alone.[^1]

---

## 5. Mechanism 4: Bone conduction and vestibular input (plausible, unmeasured)

Bone conduction during humming delivers low-frequency vibration directly to the cochlea, bypassing the air pathway that attenuates low frequencies — hence one's own hum sounds louder and deeper than it does to others.[^1]

The vestibular organs (adjacent to the cochlea) also receive this vibration. Whether this produces the subjective groundedness many people report during sustained humming "has not been measured… The connection is anatomically plausible but not confirmed."[^1]

---

## 6. Mechanism 5: CSF oscillation and glymphatic enhancement (preliminary)

During humming, the closed glottis and sustained exhalation elevate intrathoracic pressure, which transmits through the venous system (internal jugular veins, dural venous sinuses) to the intracranial space, creating rhythmic oscillations of intracranial pressure synchronized with humming frequency.[^1]

Cerebrospinal fluid oscillates in response. Drehar, Kulakchevsky and colleagues demonstrated that respiratory-driven CSF flow is a significant component of cerebrospinal fluid dynamics; humming amplifies this through added glottal resistance.[^1]

Glymphatic clearance (including amyloid-beta) depends on CSF pulsatility through perivascular spaces. Humming may enhance this pulsatility. **Caveat from the source**: "that the amplified CSF pulsatility enhances glymphatic clearance during a 60-second hum has not been directly measured. The physics supports the connection. The direct evidence does not yet exist."[^1]

## 8. Practical notes

- No technique required. A relaxed, natural, mid-range hum is optimal; the note that feels easiest to sustain is already close to sinus resonant frequency.
- 60 seconds is sufficient to activate all confirmed mechanisms.
- Humming before sleep simultaneously activates sinus NO flushing, dual vagal stimulation, and enforced exhale-dominant breathing — without counting, training, or conscious breath control.[^1]
