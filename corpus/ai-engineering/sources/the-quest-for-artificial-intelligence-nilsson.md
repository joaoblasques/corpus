# The Quest for Artificial Intelligence — Nils Nilsson (Cambridge, 2010)

TL;DR: Nils Nilsson's 707-page history of AI (Cambridge University Press, 2010) is the canonical insider account of the field, written by a 50-year participant at SRI and Stanford. It traces AI from ancient automata myths through 1950s symbolic programs, 1970s–80s expert systems, neural-network revivals, and 2000s-era modern achievements. Freely available at [https://ai.stanford.edu/~nilsson/QAI/qai.pdf](https://ai.stanford.edu/~nilsson/QAI/qai.pdf).

## Bibliographic details

- **Author:** Nils J. Nilsson — Kumagai Professor of Engineering (Emeritus), Stanford; SRI AI Center founder; IJCAI Research Excellence Award
- **Publisher:** Cambridge University Press, 2010
- **Length:** 707 pages; 35 chapters; 8 parts
- **License:** Freely available online (Stanford faculty page)
- **Coverage:** Antiquity to ~2009

## Key themes and arguments

**The multidisciplinary synthesis argument:** AI was made possible by concurrent advances in formal logic (Boole, Frege, Russell), neuroscience (McCulloch-Pitts, Hebb), evolutionary biology (Holland), and computer engineering (Turing, von Neumann). No single discipline sufficed; AI required all of them [^src2][^src3].

**The Knowledge Principle (Feigenbaum):** "The power of an intelligent program comes from the knowledge it possesses, not from the particular formalisms and inference schemes it uses." This explained the expert-systems dominance of the 1970s–80s and also predicted their brittleness — you cannot enumerate all the knowledge a person uses for open-ended tasks [^src12].

**Two competing paradigms:** The book traces a persistent tension between (1) **symbolic / GOFAI** (explicit rules, logic, search) and (2) **sub-symbolic** approaches (neural networks, statistical methods, evolutionary computation). The 1986 backpropagation paper effectively began the modern resurgence of sub-symbolic methods; statistical NLP outperformed linguistically motivated grammars by the mid-1990s [^src20][^src25].

**The "speed bumps" and cyclical funding:** Nilsson documents three periods of inflated expectations followed by funding cuts: (1) post-1969 Perceptrons critique + Lighthill Report; (2) late-1980s expert-system failures (AI winter); (3) FGCS disappointment. Each cycle accelerated a methodological pivot [^src18][^src19].

**Toward Human-Level AI:** Nilsson closes with the argument that HLAI remains AI's long-term goal; an "employment test" (machines perform any economically significant job requiring human intelligence) is more operational than the Turing test. He views the question of timeline as open but the goal as reachable in principle [^src31].

## Landmark systems covered

| System | Year | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Logic Theorist (Newell & Simon) | 1956 | First heuristic AI program; proved 38 theorems from Principia |
| Perceptron (Rosenblatt) | 1957 | First neural-network classifier with convergence theorem |
| GPS — General Problem Solver | 1957 | Means-ends analysis; goal/subgoal decomposition |
| Samuel's Checkers | 1959 | First ML program using weight adjustment + rote learning |
| LISP (McCarthy) | 1958 | List-processing language; dominated AI for 30 years |
| SHRDLU (Winograd) | 1972 | Procedural-semantic NLP in blocks world |
| A* search (Hart, Nilsson, Raphael) | 1968 | Optimal heuristic search |
| STRIPS (Fikes & Nilsson) | 1971 | Action planning with preconditions/effects |
| MYCIN (Shortliffe) | 1974 | Rule-based expert system with certainty factors; medical diagnosis |
| HEARSAY-II | 1976 | Blackboard architecture for speech recognition |
| DRAGON (Baker) | 1975 | Introduced HMMs to speech recognition |
| HARPY | 1976 | Beam search; only SUR system to meet DARPA goals |
| PROLOG (Kowalski/Colmerauer) | 1972 | Logic programming language |
| Backpropagation (Rumelhart et al.) | 1986 | Multi-layer NN gradient learning; revived neural nets |
| TD-GAMMON (Tesauro) | 1992 | RL + neural net → expert backgammon by self-play |
| Bayesian networks (Pearl) | 1985 | Probabilistic graphical models for reasoning under uncertainty |
| Deep Blue (IBM) | 1997 | Defeated Kasparov; 200M positions/second |
| Remote Agent (NASA) | 1999 | AI autonomously controlled Deep Space 1 for two days |
| Stanley (Stanford) | 2005 | Won DARPA Grand Challenge; probabilistic terrain analysis |
