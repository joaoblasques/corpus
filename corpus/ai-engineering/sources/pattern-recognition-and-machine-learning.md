# Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning (Bishop, 2006)

TL;DR: The canonical Bayesian machine learning textbook by Christopher M. Bishop (Microsoft Research). Covers probability theory through advanced inference methods, using a consistent probabilistic modeling framework throughout. 758 pages; aimed at advanced undergraduates and PhD students. See [Christopher M. Bishop](/ai-engineering/christopher-bishop.md).

## Structure (14 chapters + appendices)

| Chapter | Topic |
|---|---|
| 1 | Introduction: polynomial curve fitting, probability theory, model selection, curse of dimensionality, decision theory, information theory |
| 2 | Probability Distributions: binary/multinomial variables, Gaussian, exponential family, nonparametric methods |
| 3 | Linear Models for Regression: basis functions, Bayesian linear regression, evidence approximation |
| 4 | Linear Models for Classification: discriminant functions, logistic regression, Laplace approximation |
| 5 | Neural Networks: feed-forward, backpropagation, regularization, mixture density networks, Bayesian neural networks |
| 6 | Kernel Methods: dual representations, Gaussian processes for regression and classification |
| 7 | Sparse Kernel Machines: SVMs (max-margin), Relevance Vector Machines (RVMs) |
| 8 | Graphical Models: Bayesian networks, Markov random fields, belief propagation, sum-product/max-sum algorithms |
| 9 | Mixture Models and EM: k-means, Gaussian mixtures, the EM algorithm in general |
| 10 | Approximate Inference: variational inference, variational Bayes, expectation propagation |
| 11 | Sampling Methods: rejection sampling, importance sampling, MCMC, Gibbs sampling, hybrid Monte Carlo |
| 12 | Continuous Latent Variables: PCA, probabilistic PCA, factor analysis, kernel PCA, ICA |
| 13 | Sequential Data: Markov models, Hidden Markov Models (forward-backward, Viterbi), Linear Dynamical Systems |
| 14 | Combining Models: Bayesian model averaging, boosting, mixtures of experts |

## Key ideas

- **Probabilistic perspective throughout**: every model is expressed as a joint distribution; learning is posterior inference; prediction is marginalization. [^prml-p01]
- **Bayesian linear regression** demonstrates sequential learning: the posterior after N observations becomes the prior for observation N+1. The predictive variance shrinks monotonically as data accumulates, with the floor set by observation noise β. [^prml-p10]
- **EM algorithm**: a general technique for maximum likelihood in latent-variable models; alternates between computing the posterior over latents (E-step) and maximizing the expected complete-data log-likelihood (M-step). [^prml-p01]
- **Kernel methods** (Chapter 6–7): the dual representation connects parametric models to kernel machines; Gaussian processes emerge as the kernel limit of Bayesian linear regression; SVMs maximize the margin (the minimum distance from the hyperplane to any training point). [^prml-p01]
- **Graphical models** (Chapter 8): factor the joint distribution into a product of local factors; d-separation determines conditional independence; belief propagation is exact on trees, approximate on loopy graphs. [^prml-p01]
- **Variational inference** (Chapter 10): approximate an intractable posterior with a factorized distribution by minimizing KL divergence; ELBO is the tractable objective. [^prml-p01]

## Relationship to other corpus sources

- Foundational treatment of topics covered at applied/engineering level in other sources.
- The SVD (Brunton-Kutz) and PCA pages derive from the mathematical foundations here (Chapter 12).
- Gaussian processes (Chapter 6) underpin several AI engineering patterns (Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter search).
- HMMs (Chapter 13) are the classical precursor to attention-based sequence models covered in [Recurrent Neural Networks](/ai-engineering/recurrent-neural-networks.md) and [Attention Mechanisms](/ai-engineering/attention-mechanisms.md).
