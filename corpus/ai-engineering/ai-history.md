# History of Artificial Intelligence

TL;DR: AI's history spans ancient automata myths, 1950s symbolic programs (Logic Theorist, Perceptron, Dartmouth Project), the expert-systems boom and bust (1970s–80s), neural network revival (backprop 1986), statistical and probabilistic methods (1990s), and the modern deep-learning / LLM era. Nils Nilsson's 707-page history (Cambridge, 2010) is the canonical insider account by a 50-year participant at SRI and Stanford.

## Structure of the field (per Nilsson)

Nilsson defines AI as "that activity devoted to making machines intelligent, and intelligence is that quality that enables an entity to function appropriately and with foresight in its environment." The history is organized into eight chronological parts [^src1]:

| Period | Part | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Antiquity – 1950 | I: Beginnings | Dreams, logic, neural theory, early computers |
| 1950s–60s | II: Early Explorations | Dartmouth project, perceptrons, GPS, early NLP |
| Mid-1960s–mid-1970s | III: Efflorescence | Computer vision, Shakey robot, STRIPS planning |
| 1970s–early 1980s | IV: Applications | Speech recognition, expert systems (MYCIN, PROSPECTOR) |
| 1980s | V: New Generation | Japanese 5th Gen, DARPA Strategic Computing |
| 1980s–90s | VI: Entr'acte | AI winter, controversies, alternative paradigms |
| 1980s onward | VII: Armamentarium | Bayesian networks, ML (decision trees, neural nets, RL) |
| Present | VIII: Modern AI | Chess, driverless cars, ubiquitous AI, toward HLAI |

## Part I: Beginnings (Antiquity to ~1950)

### Chapter 1 — Dreams and Dreamers

Ancient automata myths prefigure AI: Homer's self-propelling golden tripods, Pygmalion's animated statue Galatea, Hephaestus's mechanical servants. Aristotle's conditional vision — "if shuttles could weave by themselves, there would be no need of slaves" — frames the instrumental motivation. Hobbes (1651) is "the patriarch of artificial intelligence" (per George Dyson): his *Leviathan* proposed that reasoning is nothing but computation. R.U.R. (1920, Karel Čapek) coined the term "robot" from Czech *robota* (forced labor); Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics (1942) codified the safety-alignment problem in fiction [^src1].

### Chapter 2 — Clues from Philosophy, Biology, and Engineering

**From philosophy and logic:** Aristotle's syllogisms are the first formal deductive system. Leibniz's *calculus ratiocinator* (17th c.) envisioned a universal algebraic logic. George Boole's *Laws of Thought* (1854) created Boolean algebra and propositional logic. Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879) introduced predicate calculus — first-order logic with variables and quantifiers — the notation that Robinson's resolution and PROLOG would later mechanize [^src2].

**From neuroscience:** McCulloch and Pitts (1943) showed that neurons computing logical functions could implement any Turing-computable function — the founding neural-network paper. Donald Hebb's synaptic learning rule (1949): "neurons that fire together wire together," the basis for associative memory and later Hebbian learning in neural nets [^src2].

**From biology and evolution:** John Holland's genetic algorithms applied Darwinian selection to bit-string populations, yielding evolutionary computation. Grey Walter's "tortoises" (late 1940s) were simple behaving machines; Piaget's developmental stages informed the idea that intelligence could emerge through a growth sequence [^src2].

**From engineering — cybernetics and feedback:** Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948) unified feedback control, communication theory, and biology. The flyball governor (Watt, 1788) is the canonical physical feedback example. Wiener saw goal-directed, error-correcting machines as a model for all purposive behavior [^src3].

**From engineering — computation theory:** Turing's 1936 paper defined the abstract Turing machine and proved the halting problem undecidable, establishing the Church-Turing thesis: any effectively computable function can be computed on a Turing machine. Stored-program digital computers (ENIAC 1945, Manchester Baby 1948, IAS 1952) made the practical substrate available. Turing's 1950 "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" proposed the Imitation Game (now "Turing Test") as a behavioral criterion for machine intelligence. Newell and Simon's **Physical Symbol System Hypothesis** (1976, Turing Award lecture): "A physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action" — the founding axiom of symbolic AI [^src3].

## Part II: Early Explorations (1950s–1960s)

### Chapter 3 — Founding Gatherings

Three conferences launched AI as a formal discipline [^src4]:

1. **1955 Western Joint Computer Conference** — first public session on "learning machines"; Samuel's checker-playing program demonstrated.
2. **1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project** — McCarthy, Minsky, Newell, Shannon, Simon, and others convened. McCarthy coined the term "artificial intelligence." Newell and Simon demonstrated the **Logic Theorist**, which proved 38 theorems from *Principia Mathematica* — the first program to perform a task previously thought to require human intelligence. McCarthy's advice-taker proposal: represent knowledge in first-order logic; derive actions from axioms.
3. **1958 Teddington Symposium (Mechanization of Thought Processes)** — Selfridge presented **Pandemonium**: a hierarchy of parallel "demons," each specializing in detecting a feature, whose collective "shouting" strength triggers recognition. An influential model of parallel distributed processing before the term existed.

### Chapter 4 — Pattern Recognition

- **Perceptrons (Frank Rosenblatt, 1957):** Single-layer linear classifier; the Perceptron Convergence Theorem proves it learns any linearly separable function. Rosenblatt's claims of general intelligence sparked a decade of research [^src5].
- **ADALINE/MADALINE (Widrow, 1960):** Adaptive linear neurons using the Widrow-Hoff (LMS) learning rule; applied to practical signal filtering.
- **MINOS systems (SRI):** Hardware neural-network implementations in the early 1960s.
- **Statistical pattern recognition:** Bayesian decision rules, maximum likelihood classifiers applied to character recognition and aerial-photo target detection [^src5].

Minsky and Papert's *Perceptrons* (1969) formally proved that single-layer perceptrons cannot solve XOR — a result that temporarily deflated neural-network research until multi-layer networks and backpropagation arrived in the 1980s [^src5].

### Chapters 5–7 — Heuristic Programs, Semantic Representations, Early NLP

**Heuristic search programs [^src6]:**
- **Logic Theorist (LT, 1956):** Proved mathematical theorems by heuristically selecting which axioms to apply.
- **Gelernter's Geometry Theorem Prover (1959):** Added diagram-drawing as a heuristic filter.
- **General Problem Solver (GPS, Newell & Simon, 1957):** Generalized means-ends analysis: compare current state to goal, identify the biggest difference, apply an operator to reduce it. Introduced the goal/subgoal decomposition that influenced decades of planning research.
- **Samuel's Checkers (1959):** Learned by adjusting feature weights via alpha-beta minimax search and rote-learning of board positions — the first widely recognized machine-learning program to achieve strong performance.

**Semantic representations [^src7]:**
- **SIR (Semantic Information Retrieval, Raphael 1964):** Stored relational facts and answered natural-language questions about them.
- **Semantic networks (Quillian 1966):** Graph structures where nodes are concepts and edges are typed relations; spreading activation as a retrieval mechanism.
- **Evans's geometric analogy solver (1964):** Identified geometric transforms by pattern-matching rules — an early approach to analogical reasoning.

**Early NLP [^src7]:**
- Machine translation: rule-based word-for-word MT showed immediate limitations; the 1966 **ALPAC Report** concluded that machine translation was slower, less accurate, and twice as expensive as human translation, effectively killing US MT funding for a decade.
- **BASEBALL (Green et al., 1961):** Answered questions about baseball games from a structured database — domain-specific question answering.
- **SAD SAM:** Used a semantic network to answer kinship questions.

### Chapter 8 — 1960s Infrastructure

**LISP (McCarthy, 1958):** List-processing language with symbolic atoms, S-expressions, and garbage collection; became the dominant AI programming language for three decades. IPL (Newell and Simon) was an earlier list-processing precursor [^src8].

**Major AI laboratories formed:**
- MIT AI Lab (~1959, Minsky)
- Carnegie Mellon AI group (Newell, Simon)
- Stanford AI Laboratory (SAIL, McCarthy, 1963)
- Edinburgh AI group (Michie, Longuet-Higgins, 1963)

DARPA's predecessor ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency) began large-scale AI funding following Sputnik (1957), enabling all four labs [^src8].

## Part III: Efflorescence (Mid-1960s to Mid-1970s)

### Chapter 9 — Computer Vision

**Early vision [^src8][^src9]:**
- Biological inspiration: Lettvin et al. (frog retina, 1959) and Hubel & Wiesel (cat visual cortex, 1962) showed hierarchical feature detectors — rods/cones → edge detectors → more complex patterns — motivating convolutional architectures.
- Lawrence Roberts (1963): Extracted 3D polyhedra from 2D line drawings by identifying vertices, edges, and faces — first rigorous 3D scene analysis.

**Image filtering and edge detection:**
- Roberts Cross operator (1965): gradient magnitude from 2×2 pixel differences.
- Sobel operator, Prewitt operator: more robust discrete gradient estimators.
- Marr-Hildreth LoG (Laplacian of Gaussian): biological-plausible edge detector; zero-crossings of LoG identify contour boundaries [^src9].

**Scene analysis of line drawings:**
- Guzman's SEE (1968): grouped line segments into regions by junction types.
- Huffman-Clowes labeling (1971): assigned physical interpretations (convex/concave edge, occluding boundary) to every line in a scene.
- Waltz constraint propagation (1972): extended labeling to shadows and cracks; reduced the exponential label search to near-linear by early termination — an early success of constraint propagation [^src9].

### Chapter 10 — Hand-Eye Research

Robot arms with cameras were assembled at MIT (MAC, ~1969), Stanford (Scheinman arm + SAIL vision, 1969), Hitachi (Japan), and Edinburgh's **FREDDY II** (1971) [^src9]. FREDDY II was the most capable: it could recognize, grasp, and assemble toy-block structures from a pile of parts using a head-mounted TV camera and a two-fingered hand. These systems established the sense-plan-act cycle that governs robot architecture.

### Chapters 11–12 — Knowledge Representation, Reasoning, and Shakey

**Knowledge representation milestones [^src10]:**
- **Resolution principle (Robinson, 1965):** A single refutation rule sufficient to mechanize all of first-order logic; underpinned QA3 and dozens of theorem provers.
- **PROLOG (Kowalski / Colmerauer, early 1970s):** Declarative language where programs are Horn-clause databases and computation is backward-chaining proof search; the language of the Japanese Fifth Generation project.
- **Semantic networks (Quillian, Schank's conceptual dependency):** Schank's CD theory encoded semantic primitives (PTRANS, MTRANS, ATRANS…) to represent meaning independent of surface language.
- **Scripts and frames (Schank 1975, Minsky 1975):** Scripts: stereotyped event sequences (RESTAURANT script) enabling default inference. Frames: data structures with slots, fillers, and default values — ancestors of modern object-oriented knowledge bases.

**Shakey the Robot (SRI, 1966–1972) [^src10][^src11]:**
- The first mobile robot that could perceive its environment, plan, and execute multi-step actions.
- **A\* search (Hart, Nilsson, Raphael, 1968):** Optimal heuristic search; expands the node minimizing f(n) = g(n) + h(n), where g is path cost so far and h is an admissible heuristic estimate to goal. Guaranteed optimal if h never overestimates.
- **STRIPS (Fikes & Nilsson, 1971):** Action representation with preconditions, delete-list, and add-list; the foundation of classical planning. Triangle tables and PLANEX enabled robust plan execution with recovery from unexpected outcomes.
- DARPA defunded Shakey in 1972 due to slow performance and inflated expectations; subsequent planning work (ABSTRIPS, NOAH) introduced hierarchy to reduce plan search cost [^src11].

### Chapter 13 — Natural Language Processing

- **SHRDLU (Winograd, 1972) [^src11]:** Dialog system for a simulated blocks world. Combined procedural semantics (meaning = programs to execute), systemic grammar, and a reasoning component. Could answer questions, execute commands, and explain its behavior. Impressed the field but remained locked to its micro-world.
- **Augmented Transition Networks (ATNs, Woods 1970):** Recursive-state grammar formalism for parsing; generalized to handle movement rules and gaps in English [^src12].
- **LUNAR (Woods, 1972):** ATN + semantic interpreter answering natural-language queries about Apollo moon-rock samples; the first deployed NL database interface [^src12].
- **GUS (Bobrow et al., 1977):** Frame-based conversational system for travel planning — an early dialog agent [^src12].

## Part IV: Applications and Specializations (1970s to Early 1980s)

### Chapter 14 — Expert Systems

**Dendral (Feigenbaum, Lederberg, Buchanan, ~1965–1970s) [^src12]:** Applied AI to chemistry: identified molecular structures from mass spectra using a rule-based planner. First demonstration that deep domain knowledge could substitute for general reasoning power. Feigenbaum called this the **Knowledge Principle**: "the power of an intelligent program comes from the knowledge it possesses, not from the particular formalisms and inference schemes it uses."

**MYCIN (Shortliffe, 1974) [^src14]:** Expert system for diagnosing bacterial infections. ~600 production rules of the form IF-THEN with **certainty factors** (CFs) measuring rule confidence. Retrospective study found MYCIN's antibiotic recommendations were at or above specialist level. Spawned EMYCIN, the first general expert-system shell (knowledge base separated from inference engine).

**PROSPECTOR (Duda et al., 1978) [^src14]:** Probabilistic expert system for mineral-deposit exploration. Used Bayesian belief propagation over a network of inference rules; famously helped locate a major molybdenum deposit in Washington state.

**Expert system industry (1980s) [^src14]:** Companies formed to commercialize expert systems (Teknowledge, IntelliCorp, Inference Corporation). Digital Equipment Corporation's XCON (1980) configured VAX computers and saved ~$40M/year. By the mid-1980s the global expert systems market exceeded $1B. Business Rule Engines (BREs) are the direct commercial descendants of expert-system inference engines [^src15].

### Chapter 15 — Speech Recognition

**DARPA Speech Understanding Research (SUR) program (1971–1976) [^src13]:** Funded CMU, BBN, and SRI to build systems meeting a 1000-word vocabulary, connected-speech, <10% error rate target.

Key CMU systems:
- **DRAGON (Baker, 1975):** Introduced **Hidden Markov Models (HMMs)** to speech recognition; modeled acoustic sequences as probabilistic finite automata — the approach that defined the field for 30 years.
- **HARPY (Lowerre, 1976):** Compiled all knowledge (grammar, lexicon, phonology) into a single network; used **beam search** to prune the hypothesis lattice. The only SUR system to meet DARPA's goals.
- **HEARSAY-II (Erman, Hayes-Roth, Lesser, 1974–1976):** Introduced the **Blackboard architecture**: a global shared data structure (blackboard) accessed by independent specialist processes (knowledge sources) that post, modify, and read hypotheses at different representational levels (phonemes → syllables → words → phrases). Highly influential on AI architectures beyond speech.

Subsequent work by Jelinek at IBM demonstrated that statistical language models (n-grams) dramatically outperformed linguistically motivated grammars. Dragon Systems shipped **Dragon NaturallySpeaking** (1997) — the first mass-market continuous-speech recognition product [^src13].

### Chapter 16 — Consulting Systems and NL Interfaces

**LIFER, LADDER, CHAT-80, TEAM (mid-1970s–early 1980s) [^src15]:** Natural-language interfaces to relational databases. LADDER (Hendrix et al.) queried navy ship information. CHAT-80 (Warren & Pereira, 1982) used **Definite Clause Grammars (DCGs)** — PROLOG-based grammar formalism — enabling clean integration of parsing and database query in one language.

**HASP/SIAP [^src15]:** Blackboard system (extending HEARSAY-II architecture) for acoustic ocean surveillance: interpreted sonar signals from hydrophone arrays to identify ship types and track movements. Real DARPA military application; demonstrated Blackboard beyond speech.

## Part V: New Generation (1980s)

### Chapter 19 — Japanese Fifth Generation and International Responses

**Japan's Fifth Generation Computer Systems (FGCS) project (1982–1992) [^src16]:** MITI-funded 10-year project (ICOT institute) aimed at building PROLOG-based parallel inference machines. Goal: 100–1000 MLIPS (mega-logical-inferences-per-second). Provoked major competitive responses:
- **MCC (Microelectronics and Computer Technology Corporation, US, 1983):** Industry consortium for pre-competitive AI and chip research.
- **UK Alvey Programme (1983–1987) [^src17]:** £350M government-industry program in IT/AI.
- **European ESPRIT (1984–) [^src17]:** Multi-country EU R&D initiative.
- **German DFKI (1988) [^src17]:** Deutsches Forschungszentrum für Künstliche Intelligenz.

FGCS delivered impressive parallel PROLOG systems but did not achieve general AI; by 1992 the project wound down, as neural networks and statistical methods were proving more tractable than logic-based parallelism.

### Chapter 20 — DARPA's Strategic Computing Program

**Strategic Computing (SCI) program (1983–1993, ~$1B) [^src17]:** Three flagship applications:
1. **Pilot's Associate:** Expert system to help fighter pilots manage information load; required real-time speech recognition and planning.
2. **Battle Management System:** Coordinating information from many sensors for battlefield commanders.
3. **Autonomous Land Vehicle (ALV):** Driverless military truck; pioneered real-time vision-based road-following at low speed.

Technology base investments: DARPA funded VLSI chip design, parallel architectures, expert-system tools, computer vision, and speech recognition. Nilsson's assessment: the program advanced individual AI technologies but did not achieve coupling of basic AI research to operational military systems; institutional and contracting structures inhibited basic research [^src17].

## Part VI: Entr'acte — AI Winter and Controversies (Late 1980s – Early 1990s)

### Chapter 21 — Speed Bumps

**Philosophical objections [^src18]:**
- **Lucas-Penrose argument:** Gödel's incompleteness theorems imply no formal system can prove its own consistency; ergo, human minds (if not formal systems) can "see" truths machines cannot. Nilsson's rebuttal: the argument assumes human minds are consistent — not established.
- **Searle's Chinese Room (1980):** A person following rules to manipulate Chinese symbols understands nothing; therefore symbol manipulation (GOFAI) cannot constitute understanding. Nilsson's rebuttal: the "systems reply" — the whole system (person + rules + symbols) may understand even if no part does.
- **Dreyfus's GOFAI critique:** Human intelligence depends on embodiment, tacit know-how, and situational context that cannot be reduced to explicit rules. Influenced later behavior-based robotics [^src18][^src19].
- **Weizenbaum's *Computer Power and Human Reason* (1976):** Even if AI were possible, certain decisions (psychotherapy, justice) should not be delegated to machines on ethical grounds.

**Structural limits [^src19]:**
- **Combinatorial explosion:** State-space search grows exponentially; most real problems have branching factors that exhaust any plausible compute budget.
- **P vs NP:** Many planning and scheduling problems are NP-complete; no polynomial-time algorithm known (and probably none exists).
- **Common-sense knowledge:** Expert systems were brittle because they lacked the millions of mundane facts humans use to constrain interpretation (Lenat's Cyc project was the attempted fix).
- **AI Winter:** Overpromising by researchers and vendors led to a sharp funding correction in the late 1980s; many expert-system companies folded; DARPA pulled back.

The **Lighthill Report (1973, UK):** James Lighthill's government review concluded that AI had failed to deliver on its promises in robotics and language; directly caused the UK to cut AI funding and close several labs [^src13].

### Chapter 22 — Alternative Paradigms

**Behavior-based robotics (Rodney Brooks, 1986) [^src20]:**
- **Subsumption architecture:** Stack of behavior layers (avoid-obstacle, wander, explore…) each running independently; higher layers suppress lower ones. No explicit world model needed. Brooks's "Creatures" (1986) and Herbert (1988) navigated cluttered offices without representation.

**Connectionism revival [^src20]:**
- **Parallel Distributed Processing (PDP, Rumelhart & McClelland 1986):** Two-volume compendium that revived neural-network research; introduced the backpropagation algorithm (error backprop through multi-layer networks via chain rule of calculus) to a wide audience.
- **Hopfield networks (1982):** Recurrent binary networks with symmetric weights that settle into energy minima (attractors); modeled associative memory.
- **Boltzmann machines (Hinton & Sejnowski 1983):** Stochastic Hopfield nets trained with the contrastive divergence algorithm.

**Evolutionary computation [^src20]:**
- **Genetic algorithms (Holland 1975):** Crossover + mutation on bit-string populations; implicit parallelism exploits building blocks ("schema theorem").
- **Genetic programming (Koza, 1992):** Evolved LISP S-expression trees to synthesize programs; solved analog circuit design and symbolic regression problems.

**Teleo-Reactive (TR) programs (Nilsson, 1993):** Hierarchical ordered rules with conditions evaluated on current state; bridged reactive and deliberative control [^src20].

## Part VII: Armamentarium — Methods (1980s onward)

### Chapter 23 — Reasoning and Representation

**Nonmonotonic reasoning [^src20][^src21]:**
- **Default logic (Reiter, 1980):** Extend first-order logic with default rules ("Birds typically fly, unless we know otherwise"); conclusions are not monotone — new facts can retract prior conclusions.
- **Circumscription (McCarthy, 1980):** Minimize the extension of abnormality predicates; formalize closed-world assumption.

**Qualitative reasoning [^src21]:**
- **Naive physics (Hayes, 1978):** Proposed formalizing everyday physical knowledge (liquids, solids, containers, paths) in predicate logic — an ambitious precursor to common-sense knowledge bases.
- **Qualitative process theory (Forbus, 1984):** Reasoned about physical processes without numerical values; used for device diagnosis and simulation.

**Description logics [^src21]:**
- **KL-ONE (Brachman & Schmolze, 1985):** Formal ontology language with concept taxonomies and roles; the ancestor of OWL.
- **WordNet (Miller, 1985–):** Lexical database of English synsets linked by semantic relations (hypernym, hyponym, meronym…); widely used as a backbone for NLP.
- **Cyc (Lenat, 1984–):** Ongoing project to encode millions of common-sense facts; used a frame-like KB with a large inference engine.

**Constraint satisfaction (CSP) [^src21][^src22]:**
- Arc-consistency propagation (AC-3, Mackworth 1977): before search, propagate constraints to prune domains; often reduces search space dramatically.
- DPLL algorithm (Davis-Putnam-Logemann-Loveland, 1962; extended through the 1990s): systematic backtracking for propositional SAT; modern DPLL-based solvers can handle millions of variables.
- **GSAT / WALKSAT (Selman et al., 1992):** Local search for SAT; randomly walks through assignments flipping variables to reduce unsatisfied clauses; orders of magnitude faster than DPLL on large random instances, though incomplete [^src22].

**Vector representations for text [^src22]:**
- **Vector space model (Salton, 1975):** Represent documents as TF-IDF weighted term vectors; cosine similarity for retrieval — the foundation of information retrieval.
- **Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA, Deerwester et al., 1990):** Apply SVD to term-document matrix; latent dimensions capture semantic associations; the precursor to modern word embeddings [^src22].

### Chapter 24 — Bayesian Networks

**Bayesian networks (Pearl, 1985) [^src23]:** Directed acyclic graphs where nodes are random variables and edges encode conditional independence (Bayes-net factorization). Exact inference via variable elimination or junction-tree algorithm; approximate inference via Monte Carlo sampling (MCMC, particle filters). Pearl received the 2011 Turing Award for this work.

Extensions [^src23]:
- **Automatic construction from data (Chow-Liu, Spirtes-Glymour-Scheines):** Structure learning algorithms that recover BN topology from observational data.
- **Probabilistic Relational Models (Koller & Pfeffer, 1998):** Combine first-order logic with probabilistic graphical models; precursor to Probabilistic Programming.
- **Hidden Markov Models (temporal BNs):** Discrete-state sequence models where state is latent; the Viterbi algorithm finds the most probable state sequence; EM (Baum-Welch) learns parameters.
- **Dynamic Bayesian Networks (DBNs):** Time-slice BNs for continuous temporal reasoning; generalize HMMs to multiple interacting state variables.

### Chapter 25 — Machine Learning

**Memory-based and instance-based learning [^src23]:**
- **k-Nearest Neighbor (Fix & Hodges, 1951; Cover & Hart, 1967):** Classify by majority vote of k closest training points; no explicit model; universal consistency.
- **Case-Based Reasoning (Schank, Aamodt & Plaza):** Retrieve past cases analogous to the current problem; adapt and re-use solutions; record the outcome for future retrieval.

**Decision trees [^src24]:**
- **ID3 (Quinlan, 1986):** Top-down induction using information gain (entropy reduction) to select split attributes; the algorithm that popularized decision trees.
- **C4.5 (Quinlan, 1993):** Extension with continuous attributes, pruning via reduced-error pruning, handling of missing values.
- **CART (Breiman et al., 1984):** Gini impurity as split criterion; produces binary trees; handles regression.
- **Inductive Logic Programming (Muggleton, 1991):** Induction of Horn-clause rules from examples; the intersection of logic programming and ML.

**Neural networks and backpropagation [^src24]:**
- **Backpropagation (Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams, 1986):** Efficient computation of gradients through multi-layer networks via the chain rule; published in Nature and the PDP volumes; revived neural network research after the post-Perceptrons drought.
- **NETtalk (Sejnowski & Rosenberg, 1987):** Network learned to pronounce English text from letter sequences to phonemes; trained on 1000 words, generalized to new words; demonstrated that neural nets could learn useful internal representations.
- **ALVINN (Pomerleau, 1989):** Shallow neural net trained by human driver to steer a van; ran on a CMU NavLab vehicle at highway speeds — early autonomous driving.

**Ensemble methods [^src25]:**
- **Bagging (Breiman, 1994):** Bootstrap aggregating; train many trees on resampled data sets, average predictions; variance reduction.
- **Boosting (Schapire 1990, Freund & Schapire 1997):** Sequential ensemble; weight misclassified examples more heavily at each round; AdaBoost achieves near-zero training error.
- **Support Vector Machines (Vapnik & Cortes, 1995):** Maximum-margin linear classifier; kernel trick maps input to high-dimensional feature space implicitly; soft-margin handles noise; SVMs dominated tabular-data classification benchmarks through the mid-2000s [^src25].

**Reinforcement learning [^src24][^src25]:**
- **Q-learning (Watkins, 1989):** Model-free temporal-difference learning of action-value function; converges to Q* under mild conditions.
- **Markov Decision Processes:** Formal framework for sequential decision making; value iteration and policy iteration are the classical solution algorithms.
- **TD-GAMMON (Tesauro, 1992):** Combined TD(λ) temporal difference learning with a multi-layer neural network; learned backgammon by self-play from scratch; reached expert human level — the first RL system to do so in a complex game [^src25]. See [/ai-engineering/reinforcement-learning.md](/ai-engineering/reinforcement-learning.md).
- **Aerobatic helicopter control (Abbeel, Ng, 2004):** Autonomous helicopter flew aerobatic maneuvers by apprenticeship learning from expert pilots.
- **POMDPs (Partially Observable MDPs):** Extend MDPs to settings where the state is not fully observed; the agent maintains a belief state (probability distribution over states); exact solution is PSPACE-hard [^src24].

**Statistical NLP [^src25]:**
- **Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars (PCFGs):** Assign probabilities to parse trees; trained on treebanks using inside-outside algorithm (EM); Inside algorithm sums all parse probabilities.
- **Expectation-Maximization (EM):** General algorithm for MLE with latent variables; E-step computes posterior over latents, M-step maximizes expected complete-data likelihood.
- **n-gram language models:** Predict next word from n-1 word history; simple but powerful; IBM speech group (Jelinek) demonstrated they outperformed hand-crafted grammars.

## Part VIII: Modern AI (Late 1990s to 2010)

### Chapter 28 — Extraordinary Achievements

**Game playing [^src28]:**
- **Deep Blue (IBM, 1997):** Defeated world chess champion Kasparov 3.5–2.5. Used alpha-beta search (specialized chips) at 200M positions/second, with hand-crafted evaluation function. Not learned; not general.
- **CHINOOK (Schaeffer et al., 1994–2007):** Computer checkers program; won the 1994 world championship against Tinsley (who conceded ill). In 2007, Schaeffer's team **solved** checkers: computed the optimal value for all ~5×10¹² positions, proving a draw under perfect play. First complex game proven a draw.
- **TD-GAMMON:** See above; remained competitive with commercial programs and contributed to understanding of opening theory that changed human expert play.

**Mobile robotics [^src28][^src29]:**
- **Remote Agent / RAX (NASA, 1999):** Autonomous AI planner ran aboard Deep Space 1 for two days, scheduling experiments, monitoring spacecraft health, and recovering from failures — first AI to control a spacecraft.
- **DARPA Grand Challenge 2005:** Self-driving vehicles; Stanford's **Stanley** (Thrun et al.) won the 132-mile Mojave Desert course using a probabilistic terrain classifier (PTA) trained on laser-scan/camera data correlated with vehicle accelerations from training runs. CMU's Sandstorm finished second.
- **DARPA Urban Challenge 2007:** CMU's **Boss** (Urmson et al.) navigated a 60-mile urban course with traffic, intersections, and parking — completed in 4:10 hours.

### Chapter 29 — Ubiquitous AI (2000s)

**AI in everyday products by 2009 [^src29]:**
- **Recommender systems:** Amazon's collaborative filtering ("customers who bought…"), Netflix's matrix-factorization challenge (winning team ~10% RMSE reduction).
- **Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS):** Lane-keeping, adaptive cruise, automatic emergency braking — all commercial by the mid-2000s.
- **Route finding:** Dijkstra's algorithm + A* on road networks; real-time traffic-weighted routing.
- **Computer game AI:** Behavior trees and utility-based agent architectures for non-player characters.
- **Spam filtering:** Naive Bayes on bag-of-words was the early solution; later SVMs and neural nets.

**Smart professional tools [^src29]:**
- **Medical diagnosis:** Computer-aided detection (CAD) in radiology.
- **Scheduling:** Constraint-based planners for airline crew scheduling, factory production; SAT/CSP solvers replaced rule-based systems.
- **Automated trading:** Statistical arbitrage and high-frequency trading using ML for price prediction.
- **Machine translation:** Statistical MT (IBM Models, GIZA++, phrase-based systems) replaced rule-based; later neural MT (seq2seq, 2014) and Transformer-based systems (2017).

**Face recognition:** Eigenfaces (Turk & Pentland, 1991) applied PCA to face images; later kernel PCA, LDA-based methods; by 2010, commercial face recognition was deployed widely [^src30].

### Chapter 30 — Advanced Computer Vision (2000s)

**Recovering depth [^src26]:**
- **Shape from shading (Horn, 1975):** Infer surface normals from intensity gradients under known illumination.
- **2½-D sketch (Marr, 1982):** Intermediate representation — viewer-centered depth map with surface orientation — between image and 3D object model.
- **Intrinsic images (Barrow & Tenenbaum, 1978):** Decompose image into reflectance and illumination layers.
- **Stereo, structure-from-motion, depth from defocus:** Geometric cues for 3D recovery.

**Probabilistic tracking [^src26]:**
- **Condensation algorithm / Particle filters (Isard & Blake, 1998):** Monte Carlo sampling of the posterior state distribution for visual contour tracking; handled multimodal distributions and occlusion.
- **Kalman filter:** Linear Gaussian tracking; widely used in robotics (SLAM) and target tracking.

**Hierarchical cortex-inspired models [^src26]:**
- **Deep belief networks (Hinton et al., 2006):** Stacked RBMs pre-trained greedily layer-by-layer; fine-tuned with backprop. The paper that restarted deep learning under that name.
- **Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM, Hawkins 2004):** Cortex-inspired model: sequence memory organized in a hierarchy of columns; the Numenta platform.

### Chapter 31 — Intelligent System Architectures

**Computational architectures [^src27]:**
- **Three-layer architecture (deliberative + executive + reactive):** Separates world modeling, plan execution, and reflexive behavior; used in SIPE-2, SGP.
- **Blackboard architecture:** Hearsay-II extended to general problem solving; used in HASP/SIAP (sonar), BB1, HEARSAY-III.
- **BDI architecture (Beliefs-Desires-Intentions, Rao & Georgeff 1991):** Agent reasons about beliefs (world model), desires (goals), and intentions (committed plans); implemented in AgentSpeak(L) and the JACK agent platform. PRS (Procedural Reasoning System) was the first BDI implementation.
- **KQML (Knowledge Query and Manipulation Language, 1993):** Inter-agent communication language; influenced later MCP and agent communication standards.
- **Multiagent systems:** Auction mechanisms, game-theoretic equilibria, emergence of collective behavior; RoboCup as a standard testbed.

**Cognitive architectures [^src27]:**
- **Production systems (Newell & Simon):** Long-term memory encodes condition-action rules; working memory holds current facts; conflict resolution selects which rule to fire.
- **ACT-R (Anderson, 1993):** Cognitive architecture with declarative memory (chunks), procedural memory (production rules), and a central bottleneck (one rule fires per ~50ms cycle); validated against human behavioral data across dozens of tasks.
- **SOAR (Newell, Laird, Rosenbloom, 1987):** Problem space as the universal substrate; impasse-driven chunking turns problem-solving episodes into new production rules (compiled knowledge); used as both a psychological theory and a game-playing/robot-control architecture.

### Chapters 32–33 — Human-Level AI: Prospects and Consequences

**Toward HLAI [^src30][^src31]:**
- **Intelligent assistants:** CALO (Cognitive Agent that Learns and Organizes, DARPA PAL Program, 2003–2008): multi-university project integrating ML, NLP, and planning into a personal-assistant agent; a precursor to Siri and Cortana.
- **Learning-by-reading systems:** Programs that read text on the web and extract facts into knowledge bases (KnowItAll, TextRunner — UW, 2006–2008).
- **STAIR (STanford AI Robot):** Integrated perception, manipulation, and dialog for domestic tasks.
- **HERB (Home-Exploring Robot Butler, CMU):** Grasping and manipulation in kitchen environments.

**The Singularity hypothesis [^src30][^src31]:** Vernor Vinge (1993) predicted that superintelligent AI would arrive by ~2030, creating a "technological singularity" beyond which human futures are unforeseeable. Ray Kurzweil's *Singularity Is Near* (2005) predicted human-level AI by 2029 and merger with AI by 2045. Nilsson views the predictions as plausible but uncertain.

**Debates over HLAI definition [^src31]:**
- **Turing test critique:** Passes don't require intelligence (ELIZA effects, ChatBots); failures don't disprove it. Many researchers consider it a bad proxy.
- **Employment test (Nilsson's proposal):** HLAI arrives when machines can perform any economically significant job that requires human intelligence.
- **Staged approaches:** Turing's "child machine" proposal — train a system from scratch; Minsky's Society of Mind — intelligence emerges from many interacting agents; Brooks's embodiment-first — locomotion before language.

**Societal consequences [^src31]:**
- Displacement of cognitive workers; Nilsson surveys the historical analogy to industrial automation displacing manual workers.
- Safety and alignment: ensuring goals of superintelligent systems remain beneficial; early formulations of what would later become AI alignment research.
- Nilsson's closing: "The quest is very much still under way… I believe it will continue to animate scientists, engineers, and philosophers for a very long time to come."
