# Boring Expert Businesses

**TL;DR.** A Filter Buy founder ($23M/month) tier-ranks remote business models from F to S based on operator experience. The S-tier pick — **compliance and certification businesses** (e.g., Intertek/UL model) — wins because customers pay to *eliminate risk*, not for hours: "If you can kill the risk, you can make a killing" [^src1]. AI makes this category exceptional by handling document-heavy repetitive work while the expert still signs off and takes the risk. The meta-thesis: "The real AI opportunity isn't building AI companies. It's rebuilding boring expert businesses with AI" [^src1].

## The tier list

### F-tier — Dead models (2026)

- **Dropshipping** — no ownership of product, customer, or traffic; renting attention from Facebook/TikTok and fulfillment from a supplier; the moment ad costs rise or a competitor copies the product, it collapses [^src1].
- **Affiliate SEO** — AI flooded the internet with content; Google cut small-site traffic; ChatGPT now answers queries directly without clicks. "In 2026, it's F-tier" [^src1].

### C-tier — Works but misrepresented

- **Amazon FBA** — "$18M on Amazon last month" but rated C: "Amazon FBA is a logistics business with a marketing problem on top" with daily fires, platform risk (Amazon Basics undercutting; suppliers going direct). "You're a tenant on Amazon's land and the rank goes up every year" [^src1].
- **VA agencies** — the hire-overseas-bill-domestic-arbitrage is "a stressful people management job"; AI agents will erase the margin "in three years, probably less" [^src1].

### B-tier — Solid, recurring work

Three traits that make services durable and AI-leverageable: **recurring** (client pays every month forever), **painful** (client hates doing it; won't haggle), **document-heavy** (AI gives serious leverage — reading, sorting, extracting, flagging) [^src1].

- **Bookkeeping / medical billing / payroll** — "The most boring business on earth is also one of the best." No CPA needed to start; learn QuickBooks or Xero, pick one niche, cold email 25 owners. Medical billing is especially strong: complicated, rules change constantly, screwing it up costs the doctor real money [^src1].
- **Bookkeeping + niche specialization** can reach low A-tier [^src1].

### High B / Low A-tier — Software implementation partners

"You don't need to build software to make money in software." HubSpot, NetSuite, ServiceTitan, Salesforce all have the same problem: their software is complicated, so customers churn or underuse it. Implementation partners set it up, train teams, and ensure adoption [^src1].

The analogy: "a restaurant inside a stadium that's already sold out every night" — the vendor already sold the software and acquired the customer; you earn from the adoption gap [^src1].

Go-to-market: pick one platform (ServiceTitan for trades, NetSuite for mid-market ERP), get certified (most programs are free or cheap because vendors want certified partners), find five customers [^src1].

AI tailwind: documentation and onboarding get faster, so one expert can serve more clients; the category grows as AI proliferates more software [^src1].

### A-tier — Fractional executives

"Renting your brain to five companies at once." Every business between $1M and $20M needs senior leadership and can't afford a full-time hire [^src1].

- **Fractional CFO**: financial reporting, cash flow forecasting. $250K+/yr full-time equivalent vs. 2 days/week fractional.
- **Fractional COO**: builds systems and SOPs.
- **Fractional CMO**: sets up the marketing engine.

Rates: $5K–$15K/month per client; 4–6 clients = serious income from a laptop anywhere [^src1]. AI multiplier: a fractional CFO with AI tools can close books and run scenarios in a fraction of the time — the same person who used to handle three clients can now handle five or six [^src1].

Go-to-market for 10+ year operators: build a one-page offer, send 50 messages on LinkedIn to founders running $2M–$20M businesses, and "you'll have two clients in 30 days" [^src1].

### S-tier — Compliance and certification (the "Intertek/UL model")

The $33,600 mini-split certification quote is the case study: to sell AC units in the US, Filter Buy needed third-party certification from labs like Intertek or UL — verifying safety, electrical specs, and documentation. The quote: $33,600 for six months of work [^src1].

**The insight**: "The customer isn't paying for hours… That is not a labor bill. It was an insurance policy" [^src1]. When there's a 10% chance that a product launch fails, gets blocked, or triggers a lawsuit — a 10% chance worth millions — the customer pays to make that probability zero. **Price anchors to risk avoided, not hours worked**.

What these businesses have: expertise + a clear process + certified authority — "the most durable thing that you can sell, especially now" [^src1].

The hidden compliance economy is an entire category of businesses sharing the same DNA as B-tier (recurring, painful, document-heavy), plus one more trait: **the customer's name is on the line if you get it wrong** [^src1]. Examples:

- **Product compliance** — UL/Intertek/ETL certification; every consumer product sold in America passes through this.
- **R&D/tax credits** — cost segregation studies, energy credits, WOTC; billions sitting unclaimed because the rules are too complex for average CPAs.
- **HIPAA compliance** — every healthcare company needs it; fines start at $50,000 per violation.
- **OSHA** — every manufacturing plant, warehouse, or construction site.
- **Audit prep** — SOC 2, ISO 27001, PCI; every B2B software company needs these to close enterprise deals.

**AI's role**: handles repetitive document processing, compliance checklists, and initial review — but "the expert still has to sign off. The expert still takes the risk. The stamp still belongs to a person" [^src1]. That signature is uncommoditizable.

## The 7-day S-tier launch playbook

1. **Day 1** — Pick one workflow (product compliance, R&D credits, HIPAA, OSHA — ideally one you have experience in).
2. **Day 2** — Learn the rulebook (government or industry guidance; practitioners not gurus).
3. **Day 3** — Pick a niche (e.g., product compliance for HVAC manufacturers; R&D credits for software companies). Narrower is better.
4. **Day 4** — Build a one-page offer: what you do, who it's for, how much, how it works. "The headline should be about reducing their risk, not your hours" [^src1].
5. **Day 5** — Build a 50-prospect list (LinkedIn, Apollo, trade associations, local chamber of commerce).
6. **Day 6** — Send 50 messages (cold email or LinkedIn). "Lead with the dollar amount they're probably leaving on the table or the dollar amount they'd lose if they get this wrong" [^src1].
7. **Day 7** — Take 3 calls. "You won't close all three. You might not close any, but you'll learn more on those three calls than in three months of YouTube" [^src1].
