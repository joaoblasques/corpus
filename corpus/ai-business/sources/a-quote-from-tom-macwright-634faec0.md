# A quote from Tom MacWright

**TL;DR**: Tom MacWright observes that fully LLM-generated job application stacks — resume, portfolio, GitHub projects, commit messages — produce "accidental anonymity": the hiring reader learns nothing meaningful about the candidate as a person.[^1]

## Key observation

MacWright reports seeing applications in which every layer of a candidate's public presence has been generated: the application letter, the linked portfolio site, the GitHub projects it points to, and even the commit messages in those projects.[^1]

His central concern is epistemic, not aesthetic: "I don't know anything about these people."[^1] The signal a portfolio is meant to carry — evidence of genuine work, choices, curiosity — is absent when every artefact is synthesized.

## Accidental anonymity

MacWright coins the phrase *accidental anonymity* to name the outcome: candidates who use AI to perfect and polish every surface end up more opaque, not more legible.[^1]

> "The perfected, generated, prompted resume is generic and impersonal. It tells me nothing about this person, other than that they use particular tools."[^1]

The anonymity is *accidental* because candidates likely intend to stand out, but the homogenising effect of a shared generation process does the opposite.

## Implications for hiring

The pattern raises a signal-destruction problem on the hiring side: if the artefacts traditionally used to evaluate candidates (portfolios, commit history, written communication) can be generated wholesale, the information content of those artefacts collapses.[^1] The source does not propose remedies; it records the phenomenon as newly visible as of mid-2026.
