# "A book, a baby, and a billionaire - Noah Kagan"

> **TL;DR** Noah Kagan's November 2024 newsletter covers three topics: parenting lessons from new fatherhood, the Baselang two-sided marketplace as a replicable business model, and three strategic takeaways from the book *Kochland*.

[Source](https://noahkagan.com/a-book-a-baby-and-a-billionaire/)

---

## 1. Parenting insights

Kagan reports having "slowed down on content—one piece a month now" since his daughter's birth.[^src] He frames two lessons:

- **Partner selection as a foundational business decision.** Kagan writes that "who you choose as your partner is everything," calling shared values and partnership critical to navigating the demands of parenthood.[^src]
- **Forced prioritisation.** With available time "cut to a quarter of what it used to be," he asks himself what is truly important—using bath time as a fixed anchor to distinguish stated priorities from actual ones.[^src]

---

## 2. Baselang — a two-sided marketplace model

Baselang connects language learners with native Spanish-speaking tutors in South America. Kagan analyses it as a replicable template, not a sponsored pitch.[^src]

**Economics:** tutors earn on flexible schedules; learners pay "as little as $6 per hour (plus $9 monthly membership)."[^src]

**Why it works (per Kagan):**
- Creates value on both sides simultaneously — underutilised teaching talent matched to demand.
- Platform provides curriculum and infrastructure; actual work is done by tutors and students — comparable to Airbnb or Turo "unlocking unused resources."[^src]
- Scalable: the platform's marginal cost per additional tutor-learner pair is low.

**Generalisation signal:** Kagan suggests the model could extend to other languages, marketing, sales, or trade skills — any domain with an underutilised skill supply.[^src]

## 3. *Kochland* — business strategy takeaways

*Kochland* (Christopher Leonard) profiles Koch Industries, described as "the 2nd largest private business in the world" whose two brothers are "some of the richest people in America."[^src] Kagan extracts three strategic principles:

### 3.1 Reinvest profits aggressively

The Koch brothers "reinvested nearly every dollar back into the business instead of taking big payouts," reaching ~$44 billion each in net worth.[^src]

Kagan's heuristic: if a business generates ROI above ~10%, reinvesting beats the stock market's ~7% average; if business ROI falls short, the market may be the better allocation.[^src]

### 3.2 Track ROI and cut fast

The Kochs "meticulously tracked outcomes. If an initiative didn't produce results, they'd cut it fast. If it worked, they doubled down."[^src]

Kagan applies this at AppSumo by demanding an explicit ROI question for giveaways rather than "just hoping for great results."[^src]

### 3.3 Extend from core strengths

Koch Industries expanded from oil and gas into chemicals, paper, and trading — adjacent domains that compounded existing knowledge.[^src] Kagan frames this as leveraging an "unfair advantage": expertise, a proprietary method, or a unique resource.[^src]
