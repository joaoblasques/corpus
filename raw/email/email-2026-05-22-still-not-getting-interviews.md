Hey Joao,

Your portfolio should make it obvious what kind of developer you are, what you can build, and why someone should talk to you.

It is public proof of skill.

Recruiters and hiring managers are not going to study every project carefully.

They are going to skim, look for quick signals, and decide whether your work matches the role.

Here are 4 issues to fix if you want them to take your portfolio seriously.

1. There is no wow factor

Generic projects are easy to ignore because they have seen them many times before.

A plain to-do app, weather app, or algorithm visualizer may show that you can build basic functionality, but it does not give them a strong reason to pay attention.

You do not need to invent something completely new.

But you need a stronger angle or some kind of twist that makes the project stand out.

Instead of a plain CRUD to-do app, you could build an AI-powered meal planner that generates shopping lists or recipes. Instead of a basic stock price tracker, you could build one that uses sentiment analysis from news articles.

The functionality can still be simple, but the presentation and context make it more interesting.

2. Ambiguous descriptions/ horrible names

If they cannot understand your project in a few seconds, they will move on.

Names like Final Year Project, Cool App, or AI App do not give enough context. They may make sense to you, but they do not help them quickly understand the work.

"React Dashboard" is vague.

"E-commerce sales dashboard for real-time order tracking" is much clearer because it explains the use case immediately.

The same thing applies to the description. Keep it to 1 or 2 sentences, but make sure it explains what the project does, what tech stack you used, and what the outcome was.

For example:

"A Flask and PostgreSQL web app that tracks gym workouts and suggests new exercises using OpenAI's API."

That gives the product, the stack, and the use case without overwhelming them.

3. No role targeting

If you are applying for backend roles, the first thing in your portfolio should not be a random frontend clone.

If your portfolio has an ML model, a WordPress blog, a Python game, and a React mobile app all in the same list, it does not make you look versatile.

It can make you look unfocused.

Instead, pick 2 or 3 projects that align directly with the role you are targeting.

If you want backend roles, show an API project, a database-heavy web app, or something that demonstrates performance optimization or deployment with Docker and Kubernetes.

The point is not to prove that you have touched every area of software development.

The point is to make your fit for the role obvious.

4. Portfolio looks amateur

If your project has a website, dashboard, or full-stack interface, it needs to look professional enough to trust.

It should not feel outdated, thrown together, or copied from an old tutorial.

That does not mean you need to be a frontend engineer.

But today, AI can help you design and make things look impressive extremely fast, so there is no real excuse for having a poor-looking portfolio or website.

Make sure you use complementary colors, a modern font, consistent spacing, and a layout that works well on mobile.

Your portfolio needs projects that make your direction clear.

Make sure it proves something specific, matches the role you want, and gives you something real to explain in an interview.

If it does not do those things, it probably needs more work before it belongs in your portfolio.
