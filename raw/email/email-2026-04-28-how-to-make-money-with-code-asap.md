Hey Joao,

A lot of people keep saying there are endless ways to make money
with code in 2026.

That is true.

You can try to build a startup, grow an audience, launch a SaaS,
or freelance.

The problem is that most of those paths take longer than people
admit, and if you want extra income now, or at least soon, you
need something more direct than that.

Here is the path I think makes the most sense right now:

Use your coding skills to solve small business problems that
waste time, lose money, or create repeated manual work.

That is it.

Not a huge app.

Not your dream company.

And not some massive platform.

Just one annoying problem that somebody would gladly pay to make
go away.

To give you a clearer sense of the kind of work I mean, here are
a few simple examples:

* Setting up a lead intake flow so every form submission gets
logged, routed, and followed up properly.
​
* Automating a weekly spreadsheet process that someone on an ops
or finance team is still doing by hand.
​
* Or building a simple workflow that turns call notes into
follow-up drafts, CRM updates, and next steps, with a person
reviewing before anything gets sent.

That is the shift a lot of developers miss.

You do not need to sell coding.

You need to sell a result.

Most people do not care that you write Python, JavaScript, or
whatever stack you use.

They care that something in their business is slow, messy,
repetitive, and costing them time, and they want it fixed.

So start with problems that are already easy to see.

Look for repeated tasks.

Look for messy handoffs.

Look for work that lives in inboxes, spreadsheets, forms, CRMs,
support queues, and Slack messages.

That is where a lot of small paid opportunities are hiding.

And no, this does not mean you need to become a full-time
consultant overnight.

You only need one small offer, focused on one clear problem, with
a fixed scope for one real business, so you can turn it into one
solid case study.

That first win matters more than you think.

Because one paid solution for a real business teaches you more,
proves more, and opens more doors than months of building side
projects nobody asked for.

Once someone pays you to solve a problem, everything changes.

Now you are not just hoping your skills are useful.

You have proof.

So if you are trying to make extra money with code, here is my
advice:

Do not start by chasing every possible path.

Pick one small business problem.

Build one offer around it.

Solve it really well.

Then repeat.

Talk soon,
